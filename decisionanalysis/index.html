Mastering Strategic Decision-Making with the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP)

In complex business environments, choosing the optimal strategy requires balancing technical data, business priorities, and expert judgement. This case study explores the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), a structured multi-criteria decision framework, through the strategic planning of the EVLotus electric vehicle project.

Executive Summary

The AHP analysis for the EVLotus project provides a mathematically grounded roadmap for market entry and operational scaling. By decomposing complex strategic decisions into pairwise comparisons, the optimal business strategy was identified across customer segmentation, partner selection, product positioning, and distribution channels.

  • Priority Customer Segment: B2B transportation companies dominate with a weight of 46.62%, driven by stable long-term contracts and high revenue potential.
  • Optimal Partner Selection: Supplier B (European) leads with nearly 50% global weight, largely due to superior quality and battery technology.
  • Top Value Proposition: The E-S35 vehicle model achieved the highest performance score of 0.40, balancing affordability, technology, and charging convenience.
  • Primary Distribution Channel: A digital platform (website + mobile app) scored 0.43, making it the most scalable and effective channel for reaching customers.

What is the Analytic Hierarchy Process?

The Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) is a structured decision-making framework used to analyze complex problems involving multiple criteria. It breaks a large decision into smaller comparisons, allowing decision makers to quantify subjective judgments and determine the relative importance of each factor. Through weighted matrices and consistency checks, AHP produces decisions that are both logical and mathematically consistent.

Phase 1 — Defining Strategic Criteria

The first stage of AHP involves defining decision criteria and assigning their relative importance. In the EVLotus analysis, four key metrics determined the priority customer segment:

  • Revenue Potential (40%) — expected financial impact
  • Growth Potential (30%) — scalability over time
  • Customer Loyalty (15%) — stability of long-term partnerships
  • Strategic Fit (15%) — alignment with long-term vision

Using these weighted criteria, transportation companies in the B2B segment emerged as the strongest target market with a final weight of 46.62%.

Phase 2 — Pairwise Comparison

AHP relies on pairwise comparisons, where decision makers evaluate two factors at a time using a structured importance scale. This approach reduces bias and ensures logical consistency in decision making.

In selecting a battery supplier, EVLotus evaluated four criteria: cost, quality & technology, production capacity, and operational risk. The analysis determined that quality and technology were five times more important than production capacity, resulting in a dominant weight of 0.518. This emphasis on technological reliability ultimately made Supplier B the optimal strategic partner.

Phase 3 — Evaluating Alternatives

The final stage multiplies criteria weights by alternative scores to calculate a global performance score. EVLotus compared three electric vehicle models: E-Z10, E-S35, and E-L62 across six criteria including price, charging convenience, and digital integration.

Although the E-Z10 performed best in affordability, the E-S35 achieved the highest overall score of 0.40 due to superior technology and charging convenience, making it the optimal value proposition for the target market.

Why Use AHP?

  • Objectivity: converts qualitative judgement into measurable data.
  • Consistency: the consistency ratio (CR) ensures logical comparisons.
  • Clarity: large strategic problems are broken into manageable decisions.

The EVLotus case demonstrates that strategic planning rarely involves a perfect choice. Instead, success lies in identifying the optimal solution based on clearly defined priorities. The Analytic Hierarchy Process provides a systematic roadmap for navigating complex decisions with transparency and analytical rigor.
